How Do I Rebalance a Portfolio?

Portfolio rebalancing is the process of adjusting your investments to maintain the allocation strategy you originally intended. Over time, market fluctuations can cause certain investments to grow faster than others, changing the composition of your portfolio without you actively making any decisions.

Understanding how to rebalance a portfolio can help investors maintain diversification, manage risk, and stay aligned with their long-term financial objectives. Whether you are investing for retirement, building passive income, or pursuing capital appreciation, rebalancing is an important aspect of disciplined investing.

What Is Portfolio Rebalancing?

Portfolio rebalancing means reviewing your current allocation and comparing it to your target allocation. If certain holdings have become significantly larger or smaller than intended, adjustments may be considered to restore the desired balance.

For example, an investor who originally allocated 70% to stocks and 30% to other investments may discover that strong market performance has shifted the portfolio to 85% stocks. Rebalancing helps bring the portfolio back in line with the original strategy.

Common reasons investors rebalance include:
  • Maintaining diversification.
  • Managing concentration risk.
  • Supporting long-term objectives.
  • Preserving intended asset allocation.
  • Reducing emotional decision-making.
  • Adapting to changing circumstances.
  • Maintaining investment discipline.

Rebalancing is not about predicting markets. Instead, it is about ensuring that your portfolio continues to reflect your chosen investment approach.

How Do Investors Rebalance?

There is no single method that works for everyone. Investors often choose an approach that aligns with their goals, preferences, and investment philosophy.

Common rebalancing approaches include:
  1. Review your current portfolio allocation.
  2. Compare it with your target allocation.
  3. Identify areas where allocations have drifted.
  4. Evaluate whether adjustments are appropriate.
  5. Consider the impact of dividends and contributions.
  6. Maintain records of investment activity.
  7. Review periodically as your portfolio evolves.

Calendar-Based

Review allocations quarterly, semi-annually, or annually.

Threshold-Based

Act when allocations move beyond predetermined ranges.

Contribution-Based

Use new investments to gradually restore balance.

Goal-Oriented

Adjust allocations as financial objectives evolve.

The most effective rebalancing strategy is often the one that investors can apply consistently throughout different market environments.

Best Practices for Portfolio Rebalancing

Portfolio rebalancing is not about chasing short-term performance or attempting to predict future market movements. Instead, it is a disciplined process designed to help investors maintain consistency with their original investment objectives.

As markets rise and fall, some positions naturally grow faster than others. Without periodic reviews, portfolios can gradually drift away from their intended structure, potentially exposing investors to more concentration risk than they originally intended.

Helpful rebalancing practices include:
  • Reviewing portfolio allocations periodically.
  • Monitoring whether positions have become overly concentrated.
  • Comparing current allocations with target allocations.
  • Using new contributions strategically.
  • Considering how dividends affect allocation.
  • Maintaining accurate historical records.
  • Backing up portfolio information regularly.
  • Using dedicated portfolio software instead of increasingly complex spreadsheets.

These habits encourage consistency and can help investors avoid making decisions based solely on emotions during periods of market volatility.

The purpose of rebalancing is not perfection. Rather, it is to maintain a portfolio structure that reflects your goals, risk tolerance, and long-term investment philosophy.

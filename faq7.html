How Do I Calculate Total Return?

Learn how investors measure the real performance of their investments by including both price appreciation and dividends.

← Return to FAQ Home Page

One of the most important concepts in investing is understanding your total return. Many investors focus exclusively on whether the price of a stock has increased or decreased. However, that approach only tells part of the story.

Total return measures the complete performance of an investment by considering both capital appreciation and any income generated by the investment, such as dividends.

This metric provides a much clearer picture of how well your investments are truly performing over time.

The basic formula for total return is:

Total Return = ((Current Value + Dividends Received − Original Investment) ÷ Original Investment) × 100

Because it includes all sources of gains, total return is often considered one of the most accurate ways to evaluate investment performance.

Example of Calculating Total Return

Suppose you invested $10,000 in a stock portfolio.

After several years, your investments are now worth $11,500. During that period, you also received $700 in dividends.

Your calculation would be:

Current Value: $11,500

Dividends Received: $700

Original Investment: $10,000

Total Return = (($11,500 + $700 − $10,000) ÷ $10,000) × 100

Total Return = 22%

Even though the stock price increased by only 15%, the actual performance was better because dividends contributed an additional 7%.

This example illustrates why ignoring dividends can underestimate the true success of an investment strategy.

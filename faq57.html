Can I Use Portfolio Software for Taxes?

Yes. Many investors use portfolio software to organize the information needed during tax season. While portfolio software is not a substitute for professional tax advice, it can significantly simplify record keeping by maintaining accurate investment histories and providing easy access to important financial information.

Whether you own a few dividend-paying stocks or manage multiple portfolios containing international equities, ETFs, mutual funds, bonds, and cryptocurrencies, organized records can save time and reduce stress when preparing tax documents.

How Portfolio Software Helps During Tax Season

Investment taxes often require reviewing numerous transactions that may have occurred throughout the year. Investors may need to identify dividend income, realized gains, realized losses, and historical purchase information.

Without organized records, finding this information can become difficult, especially as portfolios grow over time.

Portfolio software can help investors organize:
  • Historical transaction records.
  • Purchase dates.
  • Sale dates.
  • Cost basis information.
  • Dividend income records.
  • Interest income records.
  • Realized capital gains.
  • Realized capital losses.
  • Cash activity.
  • Long-term investment histories.

Having these records readily available makes it easier to review information that may be useful when preparing taxes or consulting with a qualified tax professional.

What Investment Information Is Commonly Reviewed?

Tax requirements vary by country and individual circumstances, but many investors benefit from maintaining detailed records throughout the year rather than scrambling to locate documents later.

Common investment records investors review include:
  1. Total dividend income received.
  2. Interest payments earned.
  3. Historical purchases.
  4. Historical sales.
  5. Realized gains and losses.
  6. Cost basis records.
  7. Cash balances.
  8. Asset allocation summaries.
  9. Portfolio performance reports.
  10. Backup copies of investment histories.

Organized information reduces administrative burdens and allows investors to focus on understanding their investments rather than searching for missing records.
